A lot of people are setting up their own home servers to host their own services. Is it actually worth it, or just another pointless hobby that swallows your time and money?

<!--more-->

Homelabs, a term for a bundle of or just one home server, are (as the name implies) stay-at-home, always-on computer systems used to run all kinds of services, usually for oneself. You can host a huge variety of services, ranging from your own website or a collaborative Wikipedia (Bookstack) all the way to a private streaming platform (Jellyfin) or a digital document organizer (Paperless-ngx), and many more.

And the best part is you own it all! Any services you host yourself are all yours to manage and modify as you please. Your data stays in your control and not in giant companies or other people's. And since most server software is open source and free, running all kinds of services for yourself ends up costing basically nothing beyond the power bill for your server.

## Stuff is not looking good
As of late, many companies—primarily those in the entertainment sector—have been price gouging their customers to see how much they can squeeze out of them. With no other alternative but to stop watching their favorite shows completely, consumers have to begrudgingly accept the price hikes on their subscriptions and hand over the bag to keep enjoying friday movie nights.

This is happening everywhere: Microsoft just can't stop ~~renaming Office365~~ raising its prices for basic office software and cloud storage, Spotify is upping the fees on basic tiers, and NordVPN's pricing keeps going up too.

Other companies try to keep prices stable by lowering service quality or injecting ads everywhere, and that's not working either.

As you might think, this is very unfair and customers are utterly defenseless against this capitalist anti-consumer behavior. Unfortunately, there's really nothing you can do about it besides giving up that service entirely. Sure, you can cancel your Netflix subscription and hop to another, cheaper service like Disney+ or AppleTV+, but both of those companies and many more have recently increased their pricing in response to other companies increasing theirs, so you're ultimately stuck in a never ending loop where companies fight eachother to see who can milk you for more money and you're left holding the bag (the *moneyless* bag).

## Weaponized outrage
Rather than putting up with preying pricing schemes, many very smart people felt such indignation that they set out to build their own streaming service with blackjack and hookers. And make it free for people to host.

Such is the tale of Emby, a selfhosted streaming service platform that lets you stream your own movies and tv shows. Instead of hoping Netflix doesn't pull your favorite show from it's catalog, you run the server yourself and access your library from wherever you are.

And such thing happened for countless other services; for streaming there's Jellyfin, for cloud storage there's Nextcloud, for images there's Immich, and so on.

For this, you're meant to learn a few things to handle a server. But don't worry—you'll pick these up naturally as you progress.

## Conclusion
Setting up a home server is definitely worth it. Not only do you get to have control over the services you run, you also get to own your data, and be fully independent and non-reliant on big companies who don't care about the little guy.

That being said, beware, as one old laptop quickly transforms into a lighter wallet and an overkill rackmount system with dedicated networking gear, redundant storage and beefy compute. But it does give something back in the end, so I guess this new piece of equipment's cost is justified, right?
