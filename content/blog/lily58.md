I dived head-first into the split keyboard scene, and I loved it. After some months of use, I don't think I can go back to regular keyboards.

<!--more-->

![lily58](/img/blog/lily58/lily58-thumbnail.webp "A picture of my half-built lily58.")

*"What is that?"* is a question you might be asking yourself after seeing the above image.

That is a lily58—a split ergonomic keyboard designed by kata0510. It is one of the most popular split keyboards out there thanks to its *high* number of keys (I'll explain in a bit) and simple build. It's very similar to a regular keyboard too (compared to other splits), so it's easier for people who have only interacted with traditional keyboards to transition into the lily58.

After hearing about the hobby through a Discord user praising their Dactyl Manuform (another flavor of split keyboard), I have been doing some research of my own fueled by pure curiosity on what it's like to use one of these weird-looking keyboards.

![dactylmanuform](/img/blog/lily58/dactylmanuform.webp "What a Dactyl Manuform keyboard looks like. (Via u/Muster7710 on Reddit)")

Split keyboards are designed around the way human hands naturally move, so instead of forcing both hands onto a single small rectangle, they separate the keyboard into two halves and even rearrange keys to reduce unnecesary finger movement and wrist strain. There are countless split keyboards to pick from, and I chose the lily58.

## No really, what is that?!
The lily58 is not like a traditional keyboard in many ways. It's split, aligned differently, and has extra stuff like knobs and thumb keys.

To be more precise, it is:
- **Split:** Exactly what it sounds like—instead of one slab, it's two. This lets you stretch your arms out and keep your wrists straight, making long typing sessions more comfortable. It also has the added benefit of making everyone who walks by your desk ~~think you're a dweeb~~ ask "Why is it split?"
- **Column staggered:** This means that instead of having every row of keys be offset from the others, each column is aligned according to the natural length of your fingers. This way, your fingers follow a more natural range of motion (up and down, curving inward and outward in a straight path) rather than contort and bend sideways to reach keys.
- **Compact:** Most split keyboards have around 40 keys or less; with 58 keys, the lily58 is on the bigger side, yet it is still fairly small for a keyboard.
- **Tented:** Additionally, you can also tent the keyboard. Tenting is keyboard jargon for angling the keyboard so that it forms a triangle with the table. It lets your hands use the keyboard at a specific angle, keeping your forearm muscles relaxed. This lowers stress placed on the wrists, which some people say makes typing more comfortable.

On top of this, the lily58 comes with *thumb keys* and *knobs*! You really don't know what you're missing until you try it.

Thumb keys fix one big issue with keyboards; thumbs are largely useless in traditional keyboards. Back when the original layout for the traditional keyboard was designed, they decided the best fingers in your hands should be able to press exactly one key. With thumb keys, you can assign more work to your thumbs in the form of any quantity of extra keys. I personally have *layer toggles* (I'll get back to it), control, alt and space/enter assigned to the thumb keys of each side.

Knobs on the other hand are already available in a range of traditional keyboards. They're just normal rotary encoders that let the user provide incremental input, commonly used for volume control. Most keyboards come with just one (they don't really have much use other than for volume control), but the lily58, being as cool as it is, has a whopping *two* knobs!

Yes. Both do the same thing.

## The good

### Comfort
The lily58 is great at making the typing experience more comfortable. Since each half can be placed basically anywhere on your working surface, it means your hands can be positioned as you see fit. For instance, this allows me to spread my arms and straighten my back, keeping my wrists aligned with each keyboard's half instead of shoving them both together and twisting them to align my fingers with the keys.

On top of that, I also slightly tented my keyboard by 20 degrees. I doubt tenting is doing anything at all, but it makes my keyboard look like an alien artifact so I kept it that way.

### Thumb keys
Regular keyboards keep thumbs nailed to the spacebar, leaving 8 fingers free for typing. In split keyboards, all ten fingers are put to work, and the lily58 is no exception. With a generous 4 keys per thumb (5 if you include a key that's a little too far from the general resting position of the thumbs), you can make your workflow smoother by having quicker access to modifier keys through fingers that aren't needed for anything else.

### Layers
Layers are alternative keyboard layouts that are only triggered through a combination of keys, usually a modifier key plus another key. An example would be the shift layer, where if you hold down the shift key, you get access to uppercase letters. Layers are not split keyboard exclusive, but they are much more convenient ~~and necessary~~ on split keyboards. There are only so many modifier keys, so split keyboards with more than a certain amount of thumb keys will always have some that are free to use for other purposes, like layer toggle keys. With custom firmware, you can set up your own layer with your most used keys that are accessible in a pinch by holding down or pressing a layer toggle key.

This not only allows people to comfortably make use of cramped sub-40 key configurations, but also turn reasonably big keyboards like the lily58 into efficiency multipliers; as an example, I have a numpad easily accessible on my right half, and arrow keys on the homerow on my left half.

## The bad

### Firmware
Most split keyboards often have custom-built firmware pre-compiled (ready to use) and made available by their own creators. However, the default firmware comes with the most basic of settings and often times keybinds that the creator finds useful, but you might not.

So in most of the cases it is necessary for every user to edit a few lines in the firmware and configure their own keybinds, re-compile firmware, and flash it to their keyboards. And as you might know, nothing says user-friendly quite like having to completely recompile your firmware to apply a caps-lock hotfix.

It took a few days for me to fully dial in all of the keybinds I wanted in my keyboard since I had to closely watch my usage to see what I'd need most, and write it down. Then once I was ready, I sat down and threw an hour away to achieve what I wanted. 

Nowadays, some modern keyboard firmware, such as VIA and Vial, is primarily configured via graphical user interfaces, which are much more user-friendly than good ol' code-based configuration, and usually handle the firmware compiling automatically.

This is now a minor hassle, but it is nevertheless an obstacle that remains present.

### Nerd factor
Split keyboards look kinda dorky. That's a pretty big con if you ask me.

## The ugly

### Cost
Split keyboards are very niche. Not everyone knows they exist and even less people use them on a daily basis; this makes the split keyboard market not quite diverse.

When buying a split keyboard, you're left with two choices—you can either buy a DIY kit (all of the necessary parts and components required to build a unit excluding tools and will) or a prebuilt (an already-built and configured unit that arrives ready to use).

Most people tend to sway towards prebuilts of all kinds of products because building something yourself is kind of a hassle. However, the grand majority of split keyboard buyers pick up a DIY kit. Is it because they have more technical competence? Is it because they are nerds and prefer to build something themselves?

No. It is because prebuilts cost a bajillion bucks.

Remember how I said split keyboards aren't that common? Since there aren't that many customers, that means there are fewer vendors available, which means fewer parts are ordered, which means fewer technicians assembling them, which means lower product availability. All of that adds up to a $200+ dollar price tag for most prebuilts.

Building is a different story—it's cheaper money-wise but *much* more expensive time-wise. Sure, it could be nice to spend an hour or two assembling your keyboard to get acquainted with how it works and save a boatload of money, but once your "hour or two" changes into "by hour five I had something that somewhat resembled a keyboard", paying someone else to do the dirty work starts sounding like a good idea.

I'm cheap and insane though, so I got a parts kit for $25 and got to work. The build process is quite straightforward—you simply solder over a hundred components perfectly on the first try. Then, I gutted my other keyboard for switches and keycaps, and 3d printed a case and knobs. After a day of combined labor and printing time, I had my keyboard.

### Human error
No person is perfect. I am though, so that's why I swiftly soldered all 122 components onto my keyboard and immediately got to work without any issues whatsoever.

Then, ~~I realized I forgot to solder one diode~~ the stupid keyboard took a soldering iron and undid one of the diode solder joins out of pure envy of my bulletproof skills. So I had to crack it open and reflow (redo) every single diode's solder joints on my left half because the 'e' ky stoppd working outta nowhr and I got rsonably annoyd.

### Adaptation
Almost all split keyboards shuffle the physical positions of keys in such a way that you're forced to rebuild your muscle memory in order to touch type again, no matter if you keep a layout you're already used to, like the standard QWERTY. In fact, it is often recommended to switch your layout in order to separate muscle memory for standard keyboards and split keyboards.

Luckily, the adaptation period is short, lasting anywhere from a few weeks to never (some people just can't make the switch over). I personally took around 3 weeks to return to 90% of my original typing speed, and twice that to return to normalcy.

## Conclusion
The lily58 is a staple of the split keyboard community and is considered the de facto gateway drug for wonkier split keyboards. Multiple people praise it for its simple and versatile design, easy adaptation and relatively cheap cost.

However, it is a very niche piece of equipment that might not be right for you. Finding parts and building it is somewhat burdensome, adapting to it takes time, and configuring it requires a little coding knowledge. Deciding if it's worth making the jump for you depends on how much you appreciate customization on your most used tools and your time.

After my experience dailying a split keyboard, I can't go back. The lily58 did not fix my shrimp posture, or make me a 10x engineer overnight, but what it did was ruin my opinion of regular keyboards. The switch is easy, but not seamless—every time I return, it makes me aware of the limitations of what is standard, and makes me sad we have not explored anything different just because things are fine the way they are.

Unfortunately, I've now become the keyboard equivalent of a wine snob.
